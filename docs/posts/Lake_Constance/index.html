Water Level Forecast of Lake Constance

news
Author

Sina Schlegel

Published

February 25, 2025

Introduction

Water levels in large lakes are subject to complex dynamics driven by meteorological conditions, hydrology, and long-term climate trends. For Lake Constance, these fluctuations have direct operational consequences. During periods of high water levels, steeper ferry ramps complicate boarding and can restrict low-profile vehicles. Conversely, low water levels reduce cargo capacity and make loading strategies more difficult, increasing delays and fuel consumption.

In this project, we develop models to predict next-day water levels at Lake Constance using hydrological, meteorological, lunar, and climate-related data. Accurate forecasts help ferry operators anticipate issues and adjust schedules and loading strategies proactively.

Methodology

Data sources

  • Hydrological data: daily lake levels (Constance gauge) and weekly groundwater levels.
  • Meteorological data: humidity, temperature, precipitation, snow depth, pressure, sunshine, global radiation, wind speed.
  • Lunar data: daily moon illumination from NASA Horizons.
  • Climate data: annual mean surface temperature change.

All datasets are cleaned, converted to a common daily frequency, and merged by date. The final modelling period spans several decades, providing a long history with clear seasonal and interannual patterns.

Preprocessing

  • Missing values in weather variables are imputed using seasonal monthly means.
  • Groundwater and climate indicators are forward-filled to match the daily resolution.
  • Outliers are corrected using a 30-day rolling mean based only on past data.
  • The target is shifted so that features at day t predict the water level at day t+1.
  • Because levels are right-skewed, we model the log-transformed level.
  • We add seven days of lagged water levels and calendar features (e.g. month, week of year) encoded with sine/cosine transforms to capture seasonality.

For statistical models such as ARMA and linear regression, we work with deseasonalised versions of the series to ensure approximate stationarity. Stationarity is checked using standard tests (ADF and KPSS).

Models

ARMA

We fit an ARMA(p,q) model to the deseasonalised log water level and select the order via time-series cross-validation. The best configuration is an ARMA(4,3). Forecasts are produced in a rolling manner, always conditioning on the most recent data. This model already improves substantially on a naive “tomorrow equals today” benchmark.

Regularised linear regression (Ridge & Lasso)

To exploit the full feature set, we fit linear models of the form \(z_{t+1} = \beta_0 + \mathbf{x}_t^\top \boldsymbol{\beta} + \varepsilon_{t+1}\), where \(z_{t+1}\) is the deseasonalised log level and \(\mathbf{x}_t\) contains lagged levels and weather features. We use Ridge regression (L2 penalty) and Lasso regression (L1 penalty), tuning the regularisation parameter by time-series cross-validation.

Ridge regression achieves the best overall performance with an out-of-sample RMSE of about 1.40 cm, followed closely by Lasso (1.43 cm).

Random Forest

Random Forest is an ensemble of decision trees fitted on bootstrap samples of the training data. Hyperparameters such as the number of trees and maximum depth are tuned via cross-validation. The model reaches an RMSE of roughly 2.04 cm, clearly beating the naive baseline but not the best linear models.

Feature importance reveals that the previous day’s water level dominates the prediction. When we exclude this feature to inspect secondary drivers, precipitation and additional lags become the most important variables, while many other weather indicators add relatively little information for one-day-ahead forecasts.

Neural Network

We also train a fully connected neural network (MLP) with three hidden layers (32 – 64 – 32 units), ReLU activations, Adam optimiser, and L2 regularisation. The network can capture non-linear relationships and interactions between features. It achieves an RMSE of about 1.96 cm, close to but slightly worse than Ridge regression. Permutation importance again confirms the central role of lagged water levels, with precipitation as the main supporting input.

Ridge regression clearly outperforms the naive benchmark and the other models, reducing typical forecast errors by around 50% compared to simply using yesterday’s water level. Even the simpler ARMA model provides a meaningful improvement.

Conclusion

Our study shows that short-term water level forecasting for Lake Constance can be done accurately using relatively simple, interpretable models. Lagged water levels are by far the most informative predictors, with precipitation and additional lags playing a secondary role. Among the methods we compared, Ridge regression offers the best balance between accuracy and simplicity.

From a practical perspective, these forecasts can support ferry operators in planning operations, anticipating restrictions at very high or very low water levels, and communicating potential disruptions earlier to customers. Future work could extend the models to multi-day horizons and incorporate probabilistic prediction intervals to better quantify forecast uncertainty.
